POWER FACTOR METER

A power factor meter is an instrument used to measure the power factor in an electrical system. The power factor is a crucial parameter that indicates the efficiency with which electrical power is being converted into useful work output. It is defined as the ratio of real power (measured in watts) to apparent power (measured in volt-amperes) and is a measure of how effectively electrical power is being used. A power factor of 1 (or 100%) indicates that all the power is being used effectively, whereas a power factor less than 1 indicates inefficiencies.

Working Principle

A power factor meter works by measuring the phase difference between the voltage and the current waveforms. In an AC circuit, the current and voltage may not be in phase, and this phase difference causes a reduction in the power factor. 1. Voltage Measurement: The voltage sensing circuit measures the RMS (root mean square) value of the voltage.

2. Current Measurement: The current sensing circuit measures the RMS value of the current.

3. Phase Angle Determination: The phase detection circuit calculates the phase difference between the current and voltage.

5. Display: The calculated power factor is displayed on the meter.
